Suffixes

Modifiers that are methods of the text generation function created by ishml.Phrase are called suffixes. An unlimited number of suffixes may by chained together with dot notation to apply layer upon layer of modifications. Suffixes are applied from left to right. That is, the suffix closest to the right parenthesis parenthesis or closing backtick is applied to the phrase first. Then the next closest suffix is applied to that result and so on until the beginning of the chain is reached. If a phrase also has prefixes attached, the prefixes are applied first and then the suffixes are applied to the result.

Unlike prefixes, which take a hybrid approach, a suffix is either a method or a getter. Suffixes that are methods must take arguments and must include a pair a parentheses because they are defined as methods of the phrase. Others suffixes are defined as getters and the parentheses must be omitted. The use of getters improves code readablity since the phrases aren't cluttered with empty pairs of parentheses.

Combining Prefixes and Suffixes

When a phrase has both prefixes and suffixes attached, the prefixes are applied first and then suffixes are applied to the result. This sometimes leads to unintended results like example2 in the listing above. Only the last verb has the ing ending applied to it because the phrase was first turned into a list and capitalized. Only then was the phrase turned into a gerund by adding ing to the end.

We can overcome this behavior by nesting phrases. When phrases are nested, the inner phrase is evaluated first and then the outer phrase. The inner, inner phrase of example3 first applies .ing to each of the verbs. Once this phrase has been evaulated, the phrase surrounding it turns it into a comma delimited list followed by capitalizing the first letter of result. Phrases may be nested as deeply as needed.
